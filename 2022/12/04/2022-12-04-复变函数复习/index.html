Chapter 2 Cauchy’s theorem

This chapter aims to investigate properties of the integrations of holomorphic functions on simple contours.

Goursat’ theorem

The first powerful result is Goursat’s theorem, and it’s inverse Morera’s theorem is applied to proof many theorems like symmetric principle.

The statements of Goursat’s theorem is below:

If f(z)f(z)f(z) is holomorphic on an open set Ω\OmegaΩ, then for any triangle TTT whose interior contained in Ω\OmegaΩ, ∫Tf(z)dz=0\int_T f(z) dz = 0∫T​f(z)dz=0.

It is helpful to understand the trick used in the proof of Goursat’s theorem, that is splitting the contour TTT into smaller regions. The trick is used in proof of Cauchy’s integrating theorem by digging a key hole of the pole.

Note that, if fff is holomorphic on a region Ω\OmegaΩ except one point z0z_0z0​ in the interior of TTT, the result ∫Tf=0\int_T f = 0∫T​f=0 still holds. see exercise 6 chapter2.

One of applications of Goursat’s theorem is local existence of primitives. If the domain Ω\OmegaΩ of f(z)f(z)f(z) is well-shaped like Ω\OmegaΩ is a open disc.

If Ω\OmegaΩ is a region of disc and fff is holomorphic on Ω\OmegaΩ, then there exist FFF defined on Ω\OmegaΩ such that F′=fF^\prime = fF′=f.
The proof is pretty elegant and intuitive.

Further application is Cauchy’s theorem which claims that all integrations of holomorphic functions on “nice” closed contours contained in “nice” regions vanishes because of the existence of primitives. Here “nice” means the contour or regions is easy to plot like circle or disc. With this theorem, we can evaluate some improper integrals like ∫0∞sin⁡xxdx\int_{0}^\infty \tfrac{\sin x}{x}dx∫0∞​xsinx​dx and ∫0∞1−cos⁡xx2dx\int_{0}^\infty\tfrac{1 - \cos x}{x^2}dx∫0∞​x21−cosx​dx where the most important step is to determine the integrated contour like circle, semicircle, quarter and indented semicircle.
